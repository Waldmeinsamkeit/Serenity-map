# Evidence Ladder

Use this when judging claim strength.

## Strong Evidence

- Annual, interim, and quarterly reports.
- Company announcements and exchange filings.
- Exchange inquiry letters and replies.
- Earnings calls, transcripts, investor presentations with concrete data.
- Official tenders, orders, contracts, certifications, standards, patents.
- Regulator, project filing, environmental approval, energy approval, customs, or local-government documents.
- Auditable financial fields: revenue mix, gross margin, receivables, inventory, contract liabilities, operating cash flow, capex.

## Medium Evidence

- Company IR replies or product pages.
- Credible media and trade publications.
- Broker or specialist research with visible assumptions.
- Industry association reports.
- Channel checks that cite method and scope.

## Weak Evidence

- Social media posts.
- Screenshots without source.
- Rumors or unsourced channel checks.
- Price action alone.
- Vague customer or order claims.

## Needs Checking

Use this label when a claim is important but still lacks proof. Do not silently upgrade a lead into evidence.

## Claim Handling

Every important claim should be labeled as:

- `Fact`: directly supported by strong evidence.
- `Interpretation`: reasoned from facts and industry mechanics.
- `Lead`: plausible but not verified.
- `Missing`: required proof has not been found.

## Candidate Evidence Standard

For every top company:

- State what exactly it constrains or supplies.
- Give at least two concrete evidence points when possible.
- Include at least one strong source when available.
- Explain what evidence would weaken the ranking.

## Red Flags

- The company is only thematically adjacent.
- Revenue contribution is too small to matter.
- Margins do not reflect claimed scarcity.
- Receivables or inventory rise faster than revenue.
- Customers can qualify alternatives quickly.
- Capacity expands faster than demand.
- Valuation already prices in success.
